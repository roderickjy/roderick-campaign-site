Rebuilding Council Accountability: No to Lazy Politics, Yes to Structural Change

Roderick J. Young — Candidate for Hamilton City Council (General Ward)

At the Glenview Community Centre meeting, I spoke directly against Tim’s proposal for rate caps mandated by central government. Leo also raised a valid point: rate capping is lazy politics. It gives a short-term soundbite but does nothing to fix the underlying issues in council spending and decision-making. As Maxine rightly noted, it’s "lazy politics for lazy politicians."

Hamilton’s real issue is not income—it’s management. As Colin Jones pointed out, Hamilton City Council (HCC) is technically insolvent, masked only by its ability to keep raising rates. Without that power, we’d be in the same boat as several UK and US cities that have defaulted on their debts.

Colin is right: HCC has been papering over financial cracks for years. Developers are shown theoretical spreadsheets of "Development Contributions" (DCs), but the actual income is over $250 million short. Councillors are being fed rubbish information, left confused, and ultimately sidelined. The Audit Office? Asleep at the wheel for a decade.

From Staff-Led to Member-Led Governance

Mesh and I, over a simple cup of tea, discussed a structural fix:

Appoint three elected members to sit in on decisions between the CEO and senior department heads.

This committee would require two out of three sign-offs from elected representatives for decisions to proceed—especially anything involving public money or strategic direction. It’s a real-time audit of executive power, not a post-mortem.

Let’s be honest: right now, staff can—and do—wait out councillors. Over the term of seven CEOs, we’ve seen a city budget balloon from $180 million to $1.22 billion. If council doesn’t assert control, nothing changes.

This committee idea is how a new council gains back control. Not by trimming fat alone, but by restoring democratic oversight at the point of decision-making. I challenge the 12 mayoral candidates to say whether they support this reform. I’m happy to serve as the deputy mayor who helps make it real.

Rates, Productivity, and the Real Fix

While Rudi’s proposed 10% cuts across departments is one way to wake up the bureaucracy, my focus is on a 34% city-wide increase in productivity—among council staff, businesses, and households alike.

As the Binn Inn owner in Dinsdale told me:

“If my turnover increased by 34%, I could absorb rates going from $10,000 to $20,000. My fixed costs stay the same—turnover is everything.”

This is the kind of conversation we should be having: not short-term cuts or hollow caps, but economic uplift, efficiency, and better public service.
