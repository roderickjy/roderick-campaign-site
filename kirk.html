üí° Your Proposal: Targeted Water Metering ‚Üí Usage-Based Rates

Summary

  • Begin with high-use water users (e.g. large commercial or industrial).
  • Install meters at the head of each street to gather household-level consumption patterns.
  • Provide usage data to all residents.
  • Transition to a water usage-based rating system, replacing land-value based rates for water services.

‚úÖ Pros

  • User-pays model: Fairer system where people pay based on usage, not land size.
  • Demand-side transparency: Helps the public understand real usage patterns.
  • Cost-efficiency: Prioritising big users and streets before individual homes lowers initial cost.
  • Behavioural impact: Encourages conservation and reduces waste.
  • Revenue predictability: Usage-based models can be more stable and scalable once fully implemented.

‚ùå Cons / Challenges

  • Upfront investment in smart metering and data systems.
  • Privacy and technical concerns over shared street-level data.
  • Requires a phased rollout and public buy-in over several years.
  • Not a full infrastructure solution‚Äîjust part of the funding equation.

üèóÔ∏è The CCO Approach (As Criticised by Colin Jones)

Summary

  • Create a separate Council-Controlled Organisation (CCO) to handle infrastructure.
  • Use existing (and flawed) infrastructure planning methods.
  • Finance infrastructure via massive borrowing‚Äîup to 500% of annual income.
  • Add a new line to ratepayers' bills for infrastructure debt.

‚úÖ Claimed Benefits (from proponents)

  • CCOs offer greater operational flexibility and faster delivery.
  • Dedicated focus on infrastructure may improve prioritisation.
  • Can separate political interference from long-term infrastructure planning.

‚ùå Risks / Criticism (from Jones)

  • Flawed assumptions and planning from past strategies are being repeated.
  • Debt ballooning: From 280% to 500% of income‚Äîan unsustainable fiscal model.
  • Infrastructure deficit doubled from $12.5B (2021) to $28.5B (2025).
  • $3.7B shortfall in the next 10 years is unfunded.
  • Ratepayers will foot the bill without real reform or new revenue streams.
  • Line-item tax added without addressing root cost drivers.

üß© Conclusion

The water metering approach represents a bottom-up, demand-side reform that puts data in the hands of residents and introduces fairness through a usage-based model. In contrast, the CCO model doubles down on past planning failures, shifting risk and cost to the public through massive borrowing and new charges.

If Hamilton is to solve its infrastructure challenges, financial accountability and behavioural change must be part of the solution‚Äînot just new borrowing vehicles with old problems.
